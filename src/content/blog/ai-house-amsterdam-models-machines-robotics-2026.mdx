I attended the **"Models to Machines: AI, World Models, and the Next Generation of Robotics"** event at AI House Amsterdam — a packed room for a robotics show-and-tell featuring presentations, live demos, and a panel discussion.

The central theme was captured perfectly in one slide: **"AI just learned to think. Now it has to learn to move."**

For three years we have watched language models become extraordinarily capable inside the screen: writing code, drafting reports, reasoning through problems. But ask them to walk across a room, pick up a glass, or give walking directions through a city they cannot truly see, and the magic breaks.

## From Language to Physical Intelligence

The gap between digital AI and physical AI is enormous. Language models operate in a clean, structured world of tokens. Robots operate in a messy, continuous world of physics where:

- **Perception is noisy** — cameras, LIDAR, and force sensors give imperfect data
- **Actions are irreversible** — you cannot undo dropping a glass
- **Safety is non-negotiable** — a robot arm moving at speed near humans must be predictable
- **Generalization is harder** — every kitchen is different, every object has different weight and friction

World models — learned representations of how the physical world works — are the bridge. Instead of programming every possible scenario, you teach a model the physics and let it plan actions in simulation before executing them in the real world.

## ADRA and Europe's Robotics Strategy

Emanuela Girardi's presentation as ADRA president highlighted Europe's strategic position in robotics. ADRA (the European Association of AI, Data and Robotics) coordinates the public-private partnership driving AI and robotics research funding across the EU.

Europe has deep strengths in industrial robotics, automotive automation, and precision manufacturing. The question is whether it can translate those into the next generation of general-purpose robots and embodied AI agents.

## Why This Matters for Infrastructure

Physical AI workloads have fundamentally different infrastructure requirements than language models:

- **Real-time inference** — a robot needs predictions in milliseconds, not seconds
- **Edge deployment** — you cannot send sensor data to the cloud and wait for a response
- **Simulation at scale** — training in simulation (digital twins) requires massive GPU clusters
- **Multi-modal models** — combining vision, language, force sensing, and proprioception

For enterprises planning AI infrastructure, the robotics wave will drive demand for [edge AI deployment](/blog/edge-ai-deployment-kubernetes-manufacturing-2026/), low-latency inference frameworks like [NVIDIA Dynamo](/blog/nvidia-dynamo-inference-framework-distributed-serving-2026/), and specialized hardware.
